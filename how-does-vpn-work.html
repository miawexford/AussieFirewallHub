How Does a VPN Work?

Understanding the technology that protects your privacy online

Virtual Private Networks have become essential tools for online privacy, but understanding how they actually work helps you use them effectively and evaluate marketing claims critically. As someone who has spent 15 years in IT security, I've seen VPN technology evolve from corporate remote access tools to mainstream privacy solutions. Let me explain the mechanics behind this powerful technology in plain language, without assuming you have a technical background.

When you connect to the internet normally ‚Äì say, visiting a website or streaming content ‚Äì your device communicates directly with servers across the internet. Every bit of data travels through your Internet Service Provider, which can see exactly what you're accessing. Under Australia's data retention laws, your ISP must log this metadata for two years. Your IP address, which reveals your approximate location, is visible to every website you visit. This is where VPN technology fundamentally changes the equation, and understanding whether using a VPN is legal in Australia becomes relevant as you consider implementing this privacy protection.

All data between your device and the VPN server travels through an encrypted tunnel, invisible to your ISP and local network.

At its core, a VPN creates an encrypted "tunnel" between your device and a VPN server operated by your VPN provider. Think of this tunnel as a secure pipe through which all your internet traffic flows. When you activate your VPN, every piece of data leaving your device gets encrypted before transmission. This encrypted data travels through your ISP's network, but because it's encrypted, your ISP can only see that you're connected to a VPN server ‚Äì not what you're actually doing online.

Once your encrypted data reaches the VPN server, it gets decrypted and sent onward to its destination ‚Äì whether that's a website, streaming service, or online application. From the destination's perspective, the traffic appears to come from the VPN server rather than your actual device. This masks your real IP address and location. When data comes back from the destination, it travels to the VPN server, gets encrypted, and travels back through the tunnel to your device, where your VPN software decrypts it for you to use.

The Six-Step VPN Connection Process

1

Authentication and Connection

When you launch your VPN application and click connect, your device initiates contact with the VPN server you've selected. The VPN software uses your credentials to authenticate with the provider's systems, verifying that you're an authorised subscriber. This authentication process happens through secure protocols that prevent unauthorised access to the VPN network. Once authenticated, your device and the VPN server negotiate encryption parameters and establish a secure connection.

2

Tunnel Creation

After successful authentication, your VPN software creates the encrypted tunnel. This involves establishing specific protocols ‚Äì such as OpenVPN, WireGuard, or IPsec ‚Äì that define how data will be packaged, encrypted, and transmitted. Modern VPN protocols use strong encryption algorithms like AES-256, which is essentially unbreakable with current technology. The tunnel creation process takes milliseconds, though you might notice a brief pause as the connection establishes.

3

Data Encryption

Once the tunnel is active, every piece of data leaving your device gets encrypted before transmission. This includes not just website traffic, but also DNS queries (requests to translate domain names like "example.com" into IP addresses), application data, and any other internet communication. The encryption happens at the packet level ‚Äì meaning each small chunk of data gets individually encrypted, making it impossible for anyone intercepting your traffic to understand its contents.

4

ISP Transit

Your encrypted data travels through your ISP's network just like any other internet traffic. However, because it's encrypted, your ISP can only see that you're connected to a VPN server ‚Äì they can observe the amount of data being transferred and the timing, but cannot see what websites you're visiting or what content you're accessing. This is crucial for privacy under Australia's data retention laws, as the specific metadata they're required to collect becomes meaningless when all traffic goes through an encrypted VPN tunnel.

5

Server Processing

When your encrypted data reaches the VPN server, the server decrypts it and forwards it to its actual destination on the internet. The VPN server now acts as an intermediary ‚Äì from the destination website's perspective, the request is coming from the VPN server's IP address, not your real IP address. This masks your true location and identity. Quality VPN providers operate servers in multiple countries, allowing you to appear as if you're browsing from various locations worldwide.

6

Return Journey

When the destination website sends data back ‚Äì whether that's a webpage, video stream, or file download ‚Äì it goes to the VPN server. The server encrypts this data and sends it back through the tunnel to your device, where your VPN software decrypts it. This entire round trip happens rapidly, though there is some performance overhead due to the encryption/decryption process and the additional distance data must travel through the VPN server.

How VPN Encryption Actually Works

When we talk about VPN encryption, we're discussing the mathematical processes that transform readable data into seemingly random gibberish that only authorised parties can decrypt. Modern VPNs typically use AES-256 encryption, which stands for Advanced Encryption Standard with 256-bit keys. To put this in perspective, AES-256 would take billions of years to crack using current computing technology through brute force attempts.

The encryption process involves symmetric encryption, where the same key encrypts and decrypts data. When your VPN connection establishes, your device and the VPN server securely exchange encryption keys through a process called key exchange. These keys are unique to your session and change regularly ‚Äì often every few hours or even more frequently. This means that even if someone somehow obtained your encryption key, it would only decrypt a limited amount of data and would be useless shortly thereafter.

Technical Deep Dive: Encryption in Action

When you visit a website through your VPN, here's what happens at the encryption level: Your device takes the URL request and related data, runs it through the AES encryption algorithm using your session key, producing encrypted output. This encrypted data is then packaged with protocol-specific headers and transmitted. At the VPN server, the process reverses ‚Äì the server uses its copy of the session key to decrypt the data, revealing the original request. The server then forwards this request to the destination website.

Return traffic follows the same process in reverse. The encryption happens at such low levels in your network stack that it's completely transparent to applications and users ‚Äì you browse normally whilst the VPN software handles all encryption and decryption automatically in the background.

DNS Protection and Leak Prevention

One of the most crucial but often overlooked aspects of VPN functionality is DNS protection. DNS (Domain Name System) is like the internet's phonebook, translating human-readable domain names into IP addresses that computers use to connect. When you type "example.com" into your browser, a DNS query happens behind the scenes to find the corresponding IP address. Without proper protection, these DNS queries can leak outside your VPN tunnel, revealing your browsing activity even whilst you're connected to a VPN.

Quality VPN services implement DNS leak protection by routing all DNS queries through the encrypted VPN tunnel to DNS servers controlled by the VPN provider. This ensures that your ISP never sees which domains you're accessing. Some VPN providers operate their own DNS servers, whilst others use trusted third-party DNS services. Either approach works well if implemented correctly, but it's essential that DNS queries never accidentally bypass the VPN tunnel.

The Role of VPN Kill Switches

A VPN kill switch is a critical safety feature that prevents your device from accessing the internet if your VPN connection drops unexpectedly. Without a kill switch, a connection failure would result in your device reverting to normal, unencrypted internet access ‚Äì potentially exposing your real IP address and browsing activity without you realising it. This is particularly concerning if you're relying on the VPN for sensitive activities and don't immediately notice the connection dropped.

Kill switches work by monitoring your VPN connection status and automatically blocking all internet traffic if the VPN connection fails. Some kill switches operate at the application level, blocking only traffic from specific applications. Others work at the system level, blocking all network traffic until the VPN reconnects. System-level kill switches provide more robust protection but can be more disruptive if you need to disable your VPN temporarily.

VPN Performance: Understanding the Trade-offs

VPN usage inevitably involves some performance trade-off. Your data must travel to the VPN server before reaching its destination, adding distance and routing complexity. The encryption and decryption process, whilst happening at hardware speeds, still introduces some overhead. For Australian users, geographic considerations add another layer of complexity ‚Äì our distance from major internet infrastructure means that server selection significantly impacts performance.

Modern VPN protocols like WireGuard have dramatically reduced this overhead compared to older protocols. With a quality VPN service using WireGuard, you might experience only 5-15% speed reduction compared to your normal connection. However, this varies based on several factors: the physical distance to the VPN server, server load at the time you connect, your ISP's routing to the VPN provider's network, and the quality of the VPN provider's infrastructure.

Optimising VPN Performance from Australia

Choose nearby servers: When privacy is your primary goal rather than location masking, connect to VPN servers in Sydney or Melbourne. This minimises latency whilst still providing full privacy protection from your ISP.

Consider protocol selection: If your VPN service offers multiple protocols, WireGuard typically provides the best balance of security and speed for Australian connections.

Test different times: Server congestion varies throughout the day. If you experience slow speeds during peak evening hours, try connecting to alternative servers or switching to different geographic regions where it's off-peak.

Monitor background applications: VPN overhead can be more noticeable if you're already saturating your connection with downloads, updates, or cloud backups. Managing background bandwidth usage helps maintain good VPN performance.

Common Questions About VPN Functionality

How does VPN work on iPhone?

iPhones use built-in VPN support in iOS. You install your VPN provider's app, which configures VPN profiles in your system settings. Once activated, iOS routes all network traffic through the VPN tunnel automatically. The implementation is identical in functionality to other platforms, just integrated with iOS's native networking stack. The encryption, tunnelling, and server connection work exactly as described above, regardless of device type.

Does VPN drain battery?

VPN connections do consume additional battery power due to the constant encryption/decryption processing and maintaining the active connection. However, modern VPN protocols like WireGuard are highly efficient, typically adding only 5-10% additional battery drain. The impact is more noticeable on older devices with less efficient processors. For daily use, the privacy benefits typically outweigh the modest battery cost.

Can VPN be tracked?

Your ISP can see that you're using a VPN ‚Äì they can observe encrypted traffic flowing to known VPN server IP addresses. However, they cannot see what you're doing through the VPN. Quality VPN providers don't log your activities, meaning there's no record of your specific online behaviour. This is why provider jurisdiction and logging policies matter ‚Äì these determine what information could potentially be available even from the VPN provider itself.

Does VPN change IP address?

Yes, this is one of the fundamental functions of a VPN. When you connect to a VPN server, websites and online services see the VPN server's IP address rather than your real IP address. This masks your location and identity from the destinations you're accessing. Different VPN servers provide different IP addresses, allowing you to appear as if you're browsing from various locations worldwide.
